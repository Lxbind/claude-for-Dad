Lesson 06

Try Claude on a real task

~25 min hands-on, on claude.ai first real win

Up until now this course has been theory and quizzes. This lesson is where it pays off — pick the row you flagged in Lesson 5 and walk through doing it for real.

Pick something real but low-stakes — a memo you were going to write anyway, not a decision that's actually hot. The point is the feel of the tool, not the outcome. — L.

Before you start

  1. Have your workflow chart open in another tab (Lesson 1).
  2. Sign in to claude.ai in a new tab. Free signup is enough. No credit card required.
  3. Pick the row you flagged in Lesson 3 — the most obvious "claude.ai win." Examples:
    • Drafting a memo
    • Summarizing meeting notes
    • Writing a tough email
    • Comparing two suppliers from notes you'd paste in
    • Producing a one-page brief from raw notes
  4. Avoid confidential data for this lesson. Use a real task, but if it involves a specific supplier name or amount, swap them for placeholders ("Supplier A," "$X"). Lesson 4 is about feel, not stakes.

The recipe

Every effective Claude prompt has four parts. Build your prompt with them in order.

Part 1 — Role

Tell Claude who you are. One sentence.

"I'm a procurement consultant at a pharma company advising internal departments (R&D, manufacturing, commercial)."

This calibrates the tone, vocabulary, and assumptions Claude uses. Without it, you get generic advice.

Part 2 — Task

What you want Claude to do, plus the output format.

"Draft a memo to my R&D department lead explaining why we're pausing the [Supplier A] contract renewal. Two paragraphs. Tone: firm but collegial."

Specify the format. "A memo" is vague. "Two paragraphs, firm-but-collegial tone" is workable.

Part 3 — Context

Everything Claude can't know unless you tell it. Background. What's already been decided. Constraints.

"Context: Supplier A missed three deadlines this quarter. We've already discussed pausing internally. R&D was the original sponsor and pushed for them last year — that's why this needs care."

The more specific, the better. Don't make Claude guess.

Part 4 — Constraints

What not to do, or what to make sure of.

"Don't mention the missed deadlines as a list — keep it narrative. Don't apologize for the decision. Don't promise a specific replacement supplier yet."

Constraints are where most prompts get sharper. Without them you get a vanilla answer.

Put it together

Below is the full template. Open claude.ai, paste this, fill in the [brackets] for your task, and send.

Role: I'm a procurement consultant at Sanofi advising internal departments.

Task: [What you want Claude to do, and in what format].

Context:
- [Background detail 1]
- [Background detail 2]
- [Background detail 3]

Constraints:
- [What not to do]
- [Tone / length / audience requirements]

Practice the recipe here first

Before you touch real claude.ai, try the recipe in this mock. Fill in each of the four slots, then click "Send full prompt" — the mock shows how Claude would respond. Once you see the pattern, do it for real on claude.ai.

After you send

You'll get a response. Now the real work begins — iterate. Don't accept the first draft.

Reply with corrections. Specific ones. Examples:

  • "The second paragraph is too apologetic. Rewrite it without the word 'unfortunately.'"
  • "Add a sentence at the end that signals we're open to a future conversation if their delivery improves."
  • "This is too long. Cut to one paragraph."

This back-and-forth is the actual skill. The first draft is rarely what you want. The third or fourth one usually is.
